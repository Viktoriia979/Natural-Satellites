Natural Satellites:

Natural satellites are objects in space that orbit planets or dwarf planets. They are more commonly called moons. Unlike human-made satellites, natural satellites are formed by natural processes and are not built by people. Moons come in many different sizes, from small rocky bodies to large worlds with mountains, valleys, and even underground oceans. They can affect their planets by creating tides, helping to stabilise a planet's tilt, and providing scientists with clues about how the Solar System formed.

Natural satellites form in different ways. Some moons formed from the same cloud of gas and dust as their planet billions of years ago. Others were created when a large object crashed into a planet, causing pieces of rock to break away and join together to form a moon. Some moons were once asteroids or other space objects that were pulled into orbit by a planet's gravity and became captured natural satellites.

The number of natural satellites is different for each planet. Mercury and Venus have 0 moons. Earth has 1 moon. Mars has 2 moons, called Phobos and Deimos. The giant planets have many more moons. Jupiter has 95 confirmed moons, Saturn has 274, Uranus has 28, and Neptune has 16. Scientists continue to discover new moons as telescopes and space technology improve, so these numbers may change in the future.
